Research Dissertation M.Sc. Media & Communication, SPPU · 2024

Branding 'Wellness'
in India

A multimodal semiotic examination of how 10 Indian wellness brands construct identity, community, and cultural meaning on Instagram, and what it reveals about the future of health marketing in India.

01 — Overview

What this research set out to do

The global wellness economy hit $6.3 trillion in 2023, yet most academic research on how wellness brands communicate on social media remained anchored in Western contexts. Indian brands, deeply influenced by Ayurveda, regional cultural codes, and a fast-growing digital consumer base, were largely unstudied.

This dissertation analysed 50 Instagram posts across 10 prominent Indian wellness brands, applying Roland Barthes' framework of denotation and connotation alongside Kress & Van Leeuwen's social semiotics to decode the visual and linguistic strategies these brands deploy.

The result is a rich picture of how Indian wellness brands build not just products but belief systems, rooted in tradition and delivered through digital fluency.

02 — Problem

The gap this work fills

A Western-centric literature

Existing semiotic and branding research on wellness heavily favours Western markets. The cultural specificity of Indian wellness (Ayurveda, festival cycles, maternal wisdom traditions) was absent from the academic conversation.

Instagram as an understudied medium

While Instagram is the primary marketing channel for Indian D2C wellness brands, rigorous analysis of how visual grammar and multimodal storytelling work within this platform remained thin for the Indian context.

Semiotics rarely applied digitally

Barthes' denotation/connotation framework has rarely been extended to dynamic, short-form video content, leaving a methodological gap in how we analyse modern brand communication.

Brands as cultural actors

Wellness brands aren't just selling products. They're shaping norms around gender, health, and identity. This dimension had not been systematically studied in the Indian digital marketing landscape.

03 — Approach

How the research was conducted

01

Brand Selection & Sampling

Ten Indian wellness brands were selected across five categories (physical wellness, nutrition, mental health, personal care, and beauty) based on Instagram presence and engagement. Five highest-liked posts per brand were chosen from a one-year window.

02

Denotative Analysis

Each post was first read at its literal, surface level, identifying visual elements, layout, colour palette, text, and imagery without interpretive judgement. This formed the factual baseline for deeper analysis.

03

Connotative & Mythic Reading

Using Barthes' second-order signification, each post was analysed for the cultural, emotional, and ideological meanings it constructs: how colours, figures, and symbols tap into shared Indian cultural codes.

04

Social Semiotic Layer

Kress and Van Leeuwen's visual grammar was applied to examine how composition, gaze, and multimodal interaction between image, text, and sound create meaning specific to Instagram's interface and Indian audiences.

05

Cross-Brand Pattern Analysis

Findings were synthesised across all 10 brands to identify recurring semiotic strategies, shared ideological positions, and brand-specific differentiators, revealing the broader grammar of Indian wellness marketing.

04 — Key Insights

What the analysis revealed

🌿

Roots as Differentiator

Almost every brand anchored its identity in Indian tradition (Ayurveda, home remedies, festival cycles) but without heavy-handedness. Tradition was woven in subtly, not performed.

😄

Humour as Messenger

Memes, animals, and playful reels weren't just engagement tactics. They carried real health messaging. Humour lowered the guard and made difficult topics like mental health or periods more accessible.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑

Community Over Commerce

Hashtags like #CosmixFam and #CultChampions created digital tribes. Brands positioned themselves as companions in a wellness journey, not vendors, fostering loyalty through belonging.

♂️

Redefining Masculinity

Brands like Kapiva and Man Matters actively expanded the target audience for wellness to men, normalising self-care, emotional vulnerability, and Ayurvedic supplementation for male audiences.

🔍

Transparency as Trust

Brands like Plum and Cosmix made production processes visible, showing labs, sourcing, and founders on-screen. In a market saturated with claims, showing became more powerful than telling.

🧠

Holistic Framing Dominates

No brand positioned itself around a single product feature. Every brand, regardless of category, framed its offering within a broader narrative of mind, body, and social well-being.

05 — Case Snapshots

Brand-by-brand highlights

Kapiva Ayurvedic Supplements

Masculine Ayurveda: reframing ancient remedies as modern performance tools for men

Celebrity fitness icon Tiger Shroff is used to juxtapose steroids (absurd/harmful) against Shilajeet Gold (traditional/smart). Gold sparkles connote premium quality; herbal imagery connotes purity. Normalises Ayurveda for male gym culture.

Masculinity × Tradition
Mamaearth Natural Personal Care

Maternal wisdom as brand origin: the mother's home remedies, not the lab, inspire the products

Festival-set reels link beauty to acts of kindness, not appearance. The "Mama ka sikhaya" campaign taps India's deep reverence for maternal knowledge, positioning the brand as a modern extension of desi nuskhas.

Cultural Festivals × Kindness
Man Matters Men's Wellness

Emotional vulnerability as a brand value, directly challenging toxic masculinity

Minimalist white-and-blue visuals strip away distraction. Text overlays like "Men, how are you doing?" address emotional struggles head-on. Positions grooming and self-care as acts of self-respect, not vanity.

Mental Health × Gender Norms
Cult.fit Fitness Platform

Dismantling the "pain and sweat" fitness archetype through vibrant, playful storytelling

A Ranveer Singh reel begins in dark, dramatic gym tones and deliberately breaks into bright, playful colour, mocking the intensity trope. Cultfit Champions are presented as regular people, not elite athletes.

Inclusivity × Community
Be Bodywise Women's Wellness

Using humour to destigmatise women's health: periods, pain, and bodily autonomy

Powerpuff Girls memes and relatable period reels make taboo topics approachable. A key post reframes period pain as a potential medical sign, not something to simply endure, challenging a deep societal myth.

Body Positivity × Awareness
Rocket Health Mental & Sexual Health

Vox populi as authenticity: real people on real streets, not curated influencer content

Street interview reels covering sexual health, loneliness, and city-induced anxiety normalise these conversations. References to Hindi cinema (Kho Gaye Hum Kahan) connect mental health discourse to pop culture.

Destigmatisation × Pop Culture
Plum Vegan Skincare

Radical transparency as a brand pillar: showing the full formulation process on-screen

Reels invite viewers into the lab ("Formulate the gel cream with us!"), turning production into participation. Social proof figures like "Trusted by 1 million+ Bright Faces" reinforce credibility. Purple as the dominant colour connotes creativity, luxury, and individuality.

Transparency × Inclusivity
Cosmix Wellness Superfood Nutrition

Founders as semiotic assets: personal health journeys embedded directly into brand identity

Born from a co-founder's PCOS diagnosis, Cosmix positions its products as rooted in lived experience. Earthy palettes and the #CosmixFam hashtag build a community around informed, conscious consumerism over quick-fix solutions.

Founder Authenticity × Community
Healthify Me Health and Fitness App

Animal memes as empathy tools: softening the pressure of fitness goals through humour

A dog falling asleep after eating, a baby inspecting its belly after one day of dieting. These culturally resonant memes reflect real anxiety around health without judgement. The brand becomes a companion, not a critic.

Humour × Relatability
Foxtale Skincare

Nostalgia as a bridge: using pop culture memory to make skincare rituals personally meaningful

A reel using the iconic Kyunki Saas Bhi Kabhi Bahu Thi title track to prompt retinol adoption is a masterclass in cultural semiotics. Celebrity collabs with Jennifer Winget tie product efficacy to family, festivals, and emotional belonging.

Nostalgia × Celebrity Storytelling
06 — Strategic Takeaways

What brands and researchers can learn

  • Root yourself in cultural memory Ayurveda, maternal wisdom, and festival contexts are not nostalgic add-ons. They are the most potent semiotic resources available to Indian brands. Use them with precision, not pastiche.
    →
  • Let process be the product Showing sourcing, labs, and formulation processes on Instagram builds a form of trust that product claims alone cannot achieve. Transparency is a brand differentiator, not a liability.
    →
  • Design communities, not customer bases Brand hashtags and community features like #CosmixFam and Cult Champions, which signal collective identity, generate more durable loyalty than promotional mechanics. Belonging is a wellness product in itself.
    →
  • Humour is a delivery mechanism for meaning Memes and playful reels are not just engagement bait. They are the most effective container for sensitive or stigmatised health messaging. Lower the guard and the message lands deeper.
    →
  • Expand who wellness is for The most distinctive brands in this study explicitly expanded wellness beyond its traditional female demographic, normalising self-care for men and addressing male mental health with the same rigour applied to physical performance.
    →
