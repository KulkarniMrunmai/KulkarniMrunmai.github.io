Case Study Media & Diplomacy M.Sc. Dissertation

Research Dissertation · 2025

Tit for Tat Diplomacy: Framing the India-Canada Standoff

A comparative framing analysis of how two ideologically distinct Indian media outlets constructed meaning around one of the most consequential bilateral crises in recent memory.

Researcher Mrunmai Kulkarni
Programme M.Sc. Media & Communication Studies
University Savitribai Phule Pune University
Period Studied June 2023: December 2024
Framework Entman's Framing Theory (1993)
01
Overview

The Story Behind the Story

When Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau publicly alleged possible Indian state involvement in the 2023 killing of Hardeep Singh Nijjar, a Khalistan separatist, it set off a diplomatic rupture: expulsions, accusation, counter-accusation. But beneath the headlines, two very different narratives were being built by the Indian press.

This dissertation examines how The Times of India (mainstream, high-circulation) and The Wire (independent, alternative) framed identical events. What emerges is not just a study in journalism: it is a study in how nations tell stories about themselves under pressure.

Subject

India-Canada diplomatic crisis following the killing of Hardeep Singh Nijjar in British Columbia, June 2023.

Outlets Studied

The Times of India (mainstream nationalist press) vs. The Wire (independent critical journalism).

Corpus

10 articles total: 5 matched pairs, each covering a distinct diplomatic flashpoint in the crisis.

Central Finding

Both outlets didn't just report events: they actively constructed competing national realities for their readerships.

02
Problem

Media as Narrative Agent, Not Mirror

Diplomatic crises don't arrive in a vacuum: they are mediated. The press doesn't simply relay facts; it selects, frames, and contextualizes them in ways shaped by institutional loyalties, audience expectations, and editorial ideology.

In India's media landscape, the divide between mainstream and alternative journalism is especially pronounced. While TOI represents a legacy, commercially dominant press closely tied to establishment sensibilities, The Wire was founded on the promise of independent, accountability-focused reporting.

"Rather than being passive conduits of information, media outlets are active meaning-makers that selectively emphasize or de-emphasize elements of multifaceted political occurrences."

The research question, then: when covering the same diplomatic crisis, how differently do these outlets construct the problem, assign blame, issue moral judgments, and recommend solutions?

03
Approach

Methodology

A qualitative framing analysis was conducted using Entman's (1993) four-element model as the analytical lens: applied systematically across five paired article sets.

1

Problem Definition

How does each outlet introduce and define the diplomatic issue? What is the central 'problem' being communicated to readers?

2

Causal Interpretation

Who or what is assigned responsibility for the crisis? How is blame attributed: to India, Canada, diaspora politics, or external intelligence agencies?

3

Moral Evaluation

What ethical or ideological tone does the coverage carry? Are actions framed as justified, dangerous, legally questionable, or praiseworthy?

4

Treatment Recommendation

What does the coverage implicitly or explicitly advocate for: accountability, negotiation, retaliation, transparency, or defensive sovereignty?

Articles were identified through purposive sampling using advanced search methods, targeting five key diplomatic flashpoints: the initial assassination allegations, Nijjar's identity framing, intelligence discrediting, staff reductions, and leadership accountability denials.

04
Key Insights

What the Data Reveals

Across all five sample pairs, consistent patterns of narrative divergence emerged: not random, but ideologically coherent and editorially deliberate.

The Wire

Diplomatic Distancing

Consistently separated the Indian state from the assassination allegations, while still treating them as credible and serious. Cautious wording, inverted commas around official labels.

Times of India

Sovereign Defense

Framed India as a victim of biased Western intelligence and Canadian political interference. Allegations were dismissed as a "smear campaign" with little interrogation of their substance.

The Wire

Humanizing the Subject

Presented Nijjar within the complexity of Sikh diaspora politics: langar seva, community activism: questioning the automatic criminal framing applied to him.

Times of India

Criminalizing the Subject

Defined Nijjar as a "Khalistani terrorist" in its very headline, foreclosing ambiguity and legitimizing India's securitized response from the first sentence.

Both Outlets

Media as Active Agent

Neither outlet simply reported events. Both made strategic editorial choices: through word selection, source prominence, and narrative sequencing: that constructed distinct realities.

Both Outlets

Audience Identity at Play

Each outlet's framing aligned with the ideological orientation of its likely readership: critical civic readers (Wire) vs. mainstream nationalist audiences (TOI).

05
Case Snapshots

Five Flashpoints, Two Stories

Each matched pair below covers the same diplomatic event. The divergence in framing is visible from the headline itself.

Flashpoint 01: Assassination Allegations & Diplomat Expulsions
The Wire "After Trudeau Alleges Indian Govt Link to Assassination of Sikh Citizen, India, Canada Expel Diplomats"
Times of India "Tit for Tat: India Responds in Kind after Canada Expels Diplomat"
Frame Moral and diplomatic dilemma. The allegations are treated seriously. Accountability and international law are foregrounded.
Frame Defensive retaliation. India's sovereign right to respond is the story. Canada is the aggressor; India is justified.
Flashpoint 02: Identity of Hardeep Singh Nijjar
The Wire "Who Was Hardeep Nijjar, Whose Killing Has Created a Diplomatic Crisis Between India and Canada?"
Times of India "Who Was Hardeep Singh Nijjar, Khalistani Leader Killed in Canada"
Frame Complex political figure embedded in diaspora activism. The "terrorist" label is questioned, not adopted.
Frame Terrorist, defined from the first line. Identity is categorical, framing is securitized. No ambiguity offered.
Flashpoint 03: Jaishankar on Intelligence Sharing
The Wire "Canada Was Told It's Not India's 'Policy': S. Jaishankar on Hardeep Singh Nijjar Killing"
Times of India "'I'm Not Part of Five Eyes, FBI': Jaishankar on Sharing of Intelligence over Hardeep Singh Nijjar's Killing"
Frame Diplomatic transparency questioned. The gap between what was said officially and what may have happened informally is left visible.
Frame India as a sovereign outside Western intelligence alliances. External bias is the cause; sovereign rejection is the response.
Flashpoint 04: Diplomatic Staff Reductions
The Wire "Canada Slashes Local Staff in Indian Missions Due to Reduction in Diplomats"
Times of India "Citing Thin Canadian Presence, Canada Retrenches India Staffers"
Frame Political escalation and deteriorating bilateral ties. The human and diplomatic cost of the standoff is foregrounded.
Frame Administrative necessity. Political significance is downplayed. The conflict is softened into a logistical adjustment.
Flashpoint 05: Leadership Accountability
The Wire "Canada Denies Report on Modi, Jaishankar, Doval Role in Nijjar Killing; Silent on Amit Shah"
Times of India "India Rejects Canadian Media Report on Nijjar Killing, Calls It 'Ludicrous' and 'Smear Campaign'"
Frame Selective exoneration noted. The silence on Amit Shah is flagged as potentially significant. Skepticism is maintained, scrutiny is invited.
Frame Complete dismissal. India is a victim of disinformation. Vigorous reputational defense is endorsed without reservation.
06
Strategic Takeaways

What This Means Beyond the Crisis

This study's implications stretch beyond India-Canada tensions into broader questions of media literacy, press freedom, and the construction of national identity through journalism.

Media frames are foreign policy instruments.

Mainstream media aligned with governmental narratives can serve as an unofficial arm of diplomatic communication: shaping how a crisis is perceived domestically and internationally without a single press release being issued.

The same event can yield incompatible realities.

A TOI reader and a Wire reader, consuming coverage of identical events, would walk away with fundamentally different understandings of who was responsible, what was at stake, and what should be done next.

Alternative media plays an accountability role that mainstream cannot.

The Wire's willingness to humanize contested figures, flag selective denials, and maintain interpretive ambiguity demonstrates the structural value of independent journalism in democratic discourse.

Framing analysis is an essential media literacy tool.

Entman's four-element model proves particularly effective for comparative diplomatic studies: offering a replicable, structured method for decoding not just what media says, but what it does.

The war for perception is inseparable from diplomacy itself.

In the India-Canada standoff, the narrative battle fought through headlines, word choices, and editorial silences was as consequential as any official statement: shaping the public mandate for state action.
