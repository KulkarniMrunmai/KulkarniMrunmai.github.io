Research · Gender & Media Studies · 2024

All the Single Ladies: Construction of Single Female Protagonists in Bollywood, 2014–2024

A mixed-methods media study examining how Hindi cinema frames autonomy, desire, and identity for women who exist outside marriage.

Institution Savitribai Phule Pune University
Department Media & Communication Studies
Method Mixed-Methods · Content Analysis
Sample 8 Films · 2014–2024
01 Overview

The research

This study uses feminist film theory and narrative analysis to map how single women (unmarried, divorced, widowed, or choosing singlehood) are constructed in mainstream Bollywood across a transformative decade.

Why it matters

With over a billion viewers, Bollywood actively shapes cultural attitudes. How it frames singlehood for women directly influences real-world stigma, aspiration, and social permission.

02 Problem

Historically, Hindi cinema reduced single women to one-dimensional figures: the tragic spinster, the rebellious deviant, or the incomplete woman awaiting rescue through marriage. While scholarship has documented this pattern, there is a gap in longitudinal analysis spanning the post-2014 era and a near-total absence of work on how visual elements (costume, colour, setting) operate as metaphors for constraint or liberation. Intersectional dimensions of caste, class, and religion remain largely unexamined in mainstream film scholarship.

03 Approach

Qualitative lens

Textual and content analysis of dialogues, narrative arcs, and visual symbolism. Guided by Laura Mulvey’s male gaze theory, feminist film theory, and postcolonial frameworks (Spivak on widowhood).

Quantitative measures

Coded variables across 8 films: screen time, romantic subplot presence, protagonist age, education level, location type, family structure, and character shade (positive / neutral / negative).

04 Key Insights
6 / 8

Films had no romantic relationship for the protagonist, a striking departure from Bollywood convention.

7 / 8

Protagonists faced measurable societal pressure in the narrative, yet resolved their arc with autonomy intact.

Urban bias

Most progressive portrayals cluster in urban settings. Rural films like Parched remain exceptions and faced removal from OTT platforms.

06 Strategic Takeaways

  • Romance is no longer the axis. The studied films collectively decouple female identity from romantic pursuit, a structural shift that signals a new grammar for women-led narratives.

  • Progress is geographically uneven. Empowering portrayals are concentrated in urban middle-class settings. Rural and caste-aware narratives (Parched, Masaan) are outliers with lower commercial support.

  • Visual language is undertheorised. Costume, colour, and setting carry ideological weight that existing scholarship has not fully mapped, a significant opening for future media studies research.

  • OTT platforms are expanding the window. Digital-first releases face fewer gatekeeping pressures, enabling more experimental or intersectional storytelling about singlehood.

  • The cycle of stagnant representation matters. Following Spivak, repeated pitiable portrayals of widows and divorcées create a mimicking effect in society, making progressive recasting an ethical as much as aesthetic project.
